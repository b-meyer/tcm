## Overview

**Tui Na** — literally "push-grasp" — is the **third branch** of the Five Branches of TCM Treatment: therapeutic massage that works directly along the [meridian](Jingmai.md) pathways. Where [acupuncture](Acupuncture.md) reaches Qi via needles, Tui Na reaches it via hands: pressing, kneading, rolling, stretching, and percussing along channels and at acupoints. It predates acupuncture in the historical record and remains a primary modality in Chinese hospitals, particularly for musculoskeletal and pediatric work.

## How it works

Tui Na shares acupuncture's pattern framework — the practitioner diagnoses a [Zang-Fu](ZangFu.md) imbalance using [Ba Gang](BaGang.md) and [Si Zhen](SiZhen.md), then selects channels and points to work. The hands do what needles otherwise would: **mobilize Qi and Blood**, release stagnation, warm Cold patterns through friction, and physically open the soft tissues that house the meridians. The contact is typically firmer than Western therapeutic massage and is structured around named techniques (_gun fa_ rolling, _na fa_ grasping, _an fa_ pressing) rather than freestyle stroking.

- Musculoskeletal — neck and back pain, frozen shoulder, sciatica, sports injuries
- Pediatric Tui Na (_Xiao Er Tui Na_) — a distinct sub-tradition with its own point system, used for infant digestive and respiratory complaints
- Post-stroke rehabilitation and orthopedic recovery
- Internal medicine — abdominal Tui Na for digestive stagnation
- Relationship to [Qigong.md](Qigong.md) — many Tui Na practitioners cultivate their own Qi via Qigong to project healing intent through the hands
- Cupping and Gua Sha — adjunct modalities frequently combined with Tui Na
