# Qi Qing — The Seven Emotions

## Overview

**Qi Qing** — the Seven Emotions — are the **endogenous causes of disease** in TCM, paired with the [Six External Pathogens (Liu Yin)](LiuYin.md) as the exogenous causes. Each emotion corresponds to a specific organ system and produces a specific effect on Qi flow. Emotions are not pathological in themselves — they are part of healthy life — but become causative when they are excessive, prolonged, suddenly intense, or chronically suppressed.

The seven are: **Joy (Xi), Anger (Nu), Pensiveness (Si), Worry (You), Grief (Bei), Fear (Kong), and Fright (Jing)**. Their physiological effects are codified in the _Huangdi Neijing_: each one **moves Qi in a characteristic direction**, and that movement is what makes an emotion pathogenic when it goes wrong.

The framework predates modern psychosomatic medicine by two millennia and remains one of TCM's most distinctive contributions — illness arises from the body, but emotional life can damage the body just as surely as cold weather or bad food.

## The seven emotions and their Qi-direction effects

The Neijing's defining table — what each emotion _does_ to Qi:

| Emotion              | Organ          | Qi-direction effect       | Excess produces                                          |
| -------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Joy (Xi)**         | Heart          | _Slackens / loosens Qi_   | Mania, scattered thinking, palpitations, insomnia        |
| **Anger (Nu)**       | Liver          | _Makes Qi rise_           | Headache, red face, dizziness, Liver Yang rising, stroke |
| **Pensiveness (Si)** | Spleen         | _Knots / stagnates Qi_    | Digestive stagnation, fatigue, brooding                  |
| **Worry (You)**      | Lung / Spleen  | _Dissipates Qi_           | Loss of vitality, shallow breathing, poor digestion      |
| **Grief (Bei)**      | Lung           | _Consumes / dissolves Qi_ | Chronic exhaustion, breath shortness, constricted chest  |
| **Fear (Kong)**      | Kidney         | _Descends / sinks Qi_     | Urinary incontinence, weak knees, lower-back collapse    |
| **Fright (Jing)**    | Heart / Kidney | _Scatters Qi_             | Sudden palpitations, dissociation, confusion             |

The distinction between _fear_ (chronic, slow-acting, sinks Qi) and _fright_ (sudden shock, scatters Qi) is clinically important. So is the distinction between _worry_ (a low-grade ongoing anxiety, dissipates) and _pensiveness_ (rumination that goes in circles, knots).

## How emotions cause disease

Emotions become pathogens through three mechanisms:

1. **Direct damage to their associated organ.** Chronic anger damages the Liver; chronic worry weakens the Spleen and Lung. Each organ has its tolerance limit.
2. **Qi-direction disruption.** When the Qi-direction effect becomes severe or chronic, normal Qi flow throughout the body breaks down. Anger driving Qi upward produces hypertension and headache; fear driving it downward produces incontinence. Disordered Qi then begets disordered Blood, fluids, and organ function downstream.
3. **Shen disturbance.** The Heart houses Shen (see [Shen.md](Shen.md)); strong emotion of any kind perturbs Shen, and unsettled Shen disrupts every other organ. Long-standing emotional disturbance always presents with some degree of Shen disharmony.

A crucial TCM principle: **suppressed and expressed emotions are both pathological in different ways**. Unexpressed anger doesn't dissolve — it transforms into Liver Qi stagnation, which over time becomes Liver Fire or attacks the Spleen (the classic stress-and-IBS pattern, see [ZangFu.md](ZangFu.md)). Catharsis is not the answer either; explosively expressed anger still drives Qi upward and damages the Liver. The middle path is _appropriate_ emotion: feeling, processing, and letting go.

## The Five Phases overlay

The Seven Emotions map onto five of the [Five Phases](WuXing.md) (Worry and Pensiveness collapse into the Spleen group; Grief is sometimes paired with Worry under Metal):

| Phase | Emotion               | Organ  |
| ----- | --------------------- | ------ |
| Wood  | Anger / Frustration   | Liver  |
| Fire  | Joy / Over-excitement | Heart  |
| Earth | Pensiveness / Worry   | Spleen |
| Metal | Grief / Sadness       | Lung   |
| Water | Fear / Fright         | Kidney |

The same five organ-emotion pairings carry the Five Shen (sub-spirits) framework — Heart-Shen, Liver-Hun, Spleen-Yi, Lung-Po, Kidney-Zhi — covered in depth in [Shen.md](Shen.md). Where this file traces _how_ emotions damage organs, Shen.md traces _which aspect of consciousness_ each organ houses.

This puts emotions into the generating and controlling cycles. Clinically the **controlling cycle of emotions** is a treatment principle: one emotion can be deliberately invoked to counteract another excess. Anger (Wood) controls Pensiveness (Earth); Joy (Fire) controls Grief (Metal); Pensiveness (Earth) controls Fear (Water); Sadness (Metal) controls Anger (Wood); Fear (Water) controls Joy (Fire). The classical _qing zhi xiang sheng_ — counter-emotion therapy — was used by physicians to break stuck patterns: a patient frozen in grief might be carefully provoked into anger to mobilize stuck Qi.

## Suppression vs expression

TCM is unusual in framing both suppression _and_ unchecked expression as pathological. The healthy stance is closer to Aristotelian moderation than to Western therapeutic catharsis:

- **Suppressed emotion** → stagnation. Suppressed anger → Liver Qi stagnation → headache, PMS, IBS. Suppressed grief → Lung Qi deficiency → susceptibility to colds, shallow breath.
- **Expressed but uncontained emotion** → damage. Explosive anger → Liver Yang rising → stroke, hypertension. Excessive joy (mania) → Heart Fire → insomnia, palpitations.
- **Healthy emotion** → moves and resolves. The feeling arises, is acknowledged, runs its course, and dissipates. The body returns to baseline.

## Common emotional patterns in practice

A few patterns recur frequently enough to have named formulas:

- **Liver Qi stagnation from suppressed frustration** — the most-diagnosed emotional pattern in modern practice. Plum-pit throat sensation, rib-side tension, sighing, irritability, PMS. _Xiao Yao San_ (Free and Easy Wanderer) is the canonical formula.
- **Heart-Spleen Blood deficiency from prolonged worry** — overworking, caretaker burnout, women postpartum. Insomnia, anxiety, palpitations, poor appetite, pale tongue. _Gui Pi Tang_ (Restore the Spleen) addresses both organs simultaneously.
- **Heart-Kidney non-communication from chronic fear** — anxiety with insomnia, night sweats, lumbar weakness. Fire and Water fail to dialogue (see [ZangFu.md](ZangFu.md)). _Tian Wang Bu Xin Dan_ is canonical.
- **Lung Qi consumed by unresolved grief** — chronic sadness following loss, shallow breath, low immunity, dry cough. Often months to years after the precipitating event.

## Diagnostic markers

Emotional patterns are read across the standard diagnostic windows (see [SiZhen.md](SiZhen.md)):

- **Pulse.** A _wiry_ pulse is the cardinal sign of Liver Qi stagnation (suppressed frustration). A _thin_ pulse with poor strength indicates chronic worry depleting the Spleen and Heart. A _deep weak_ pulse at the chi (proximal) position suggests fear depleting the Kidneys.
- **Tongue.** Red sides point to Liver Heat (chronic anger or stagnation transforming). Pale and scalloped suggests Spleen depletion from worry. A red tongue tip indicates Heart Fire — often joy excess or sustained agitation. Dusky purple anywhere suggests long-standing Qi stagnation moving toward Xue Stasis.
- **Voice and breath.** Frequent sighing is diagnostic of Liver Qi stagnation almost on its own. A weak, soft voice with poor breath support reflects Lung Qi consumed by grief.
- **Eyes.** Strong Shen in the eyes is preserved even in distressed patients with intact constitutions. Lost or "vacant" Shen signals deeper depletion than the presenting emotional complaint suggests.

## Treatment principles

Emotional patterns rarely yield to herbs alone; treatment is multimodal:

- **Move the stuck Qi** — herbs and acupoints that course Liver Qi (the dominant target), regulate the chest, soothe the Heart.
- **Nourish the depleted substance** — Blood deficiency from chronic worry needs Blood-building; Yin deficiency from prolonged fear needs Yin-nourishing.
- **Calm the Shen** — see [Shen.md](Shen.md) for points like HT 7 (Shen Men), Yin Tang, An Mian.
- **Counter-emotion therapy** _(qing zhi xiang sheng)_ — strategic use of one emotion to unstick another; applied gently and judiciously.
- **Lifestyle change** — addressing the source. TCM has always understood that recurring stress is medicine's most challenging adversary, since the cause continues to generate the pattern.

## Modern parallels

The Qi Qing framework anticipates several modern concepts:

- **Psychosomatic medicine** maps directly: emotional states cause demonstrable physiological change. The TCM framework names _which_ states cause _which_ changes with surprising specificity.
- **Gut-brain axis** parallels the Spleen-Heart connection — chronic anxiety and digestive dysfunction co-occurring is exactly what TCM predicts when worry damages Spleen and Shen simultaneously.
- **HPA axis dysregulation** in chronic stress overlaps with the Liver-Qi-stagnation-progressing-to-Heat trajectory and with Kidney Yang depletion from prolonged fear states.
- **Polyvagal theory** echoes the TCM observation that fright scatters Qi while chronic fear sinks it — different stress responses producing different physiology.
- **Affect-organ specificity research** in psychoneuroimmunology has begun to support what TCM has long claimed: specific emotions correlate with specific organ-system effects, not merely generic "stress."

The framework's enduring usefulness is that it gives the physician a **vocabulary for the emotional cause without reducing the patient to a psychiatric label**. A patient with Liver Qi stagnation is not a "depressed person" — they are someone whose system is bottling up movement that needs to flow. The treatment addresses the body, and the body's release supports the mind's.
