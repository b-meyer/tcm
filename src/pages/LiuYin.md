# Liu Yin — The Six External Pathogens

## Overview

**Liu Yin** — literally "the six excesses" — names the environmental forces that cross from being normal climatic factors into pathogenic agents when they overwhelm the body's defenses. They are the **exogenous causes of disease** in classical TCM, paired with the [Seven Emotions (QiQing)](QiQing.md) as the endogenous causes.

The six evils are **Wind, Cold, Heat/Fire, Damp, Dryness, and Summer-Heat**. Each corresponds to a seasonal influence (Wind = spring, Heat = summer, Damp = late summer, Dryness = autumn, Cold = winter), and each invades through specific entry routes with specific symptom patterns. In TCM, illness is rarely "an infection" in the abstract — it is _which evil entered, where, and how deep_.

The framework distinguishes **external evils** (climate-driven, arrive from outside) from **internal evils** (arise from organ-system failure but produce identical patterns). External Damp comes from humid weather; internal Damp comes from Spleen failure — the symptoms overlap, but the treatment is fundamentally different.

## The defense hierarchy

The body's defense rests on three layers of Qi:

1. **Wei Qi** (Defensive Qi) — patrols just beneath the skin, generated by Lung in concert with Kidney; the first line. Strong Wei Qi prevents invasion entirely; weak Wei Qi lets evils through (see [Qi.md](Qi.md)).
2. **Zheng Qi** (Upright Qi) — the body's overall constitutional strength; what an evil pathogen must defeat to establish itself.
3. **Anti-pathogenic capacity** at the level of each organ system.

The classical principle: _xie zhi suo cou, qi qi bi xu_ — "where evil gathers, the body's Qi must be deficient." Pathogens do not invade strong systems.

## The Six Evils

### Wind (Feng)

**The leader of the hundred diseases.** Wind is the gateway evil; the other five typically ride in with it (Wind-Cold, Wind-Heat, Wind-Damp). Its qualities are **sudden onset, shifting location, upward and outward movement, and rapid change**.

- **Entry route:** nape of the neck, upper back, face.
- **Hallmark signs:** sudden onset, fever with mild chills, headache, body aches that shift, sneezing, itching that moves.
- **Pattern affinity:** Lung and skin (the body's exterior).
- **Internal Wind** is a different pattern: it arises from Liver Yang rising or Liver Blood deficiency and produces tremors, convulsions, dizziness, sudden facial paralysis, or stroke. Modern integrative practitioners often map Internal Wind onto neurological events and certain autoimmune flares.

### Cold (Han)

Cold **constricts, slows, and contracts**. It is _yin_ in nature and consumes Yang Qi.

- **Entry route:** skin, lower back, and abdomen.
- **Hallmark signs:** chills, pale complexion, clear nasal discharge, sharp fixed pain that worsens with cold and improves with warmth, watery stools, frequent clear urination, slow tight pulse.
- **Pattern affinity:** Spleen (digestion), Kidney (lower back), Lung (Wind-Cold colds).
- **Internal Cold** arises from Yang deficiency — Kidney Yang or Spleen Yang failing to warm the body. The picture matches external Cold but is constitutional, chronic, and worsens with cold weather rather than triggering with it.

### Heat / Fire (Re / Huo)

Heat **accelerates, dries, and ascends.** It is _yang_ in nature and consumes both Yin fluids and Blood. Fire is intense Heat — same nature, more severe.

- **Entry route:** mouth, nose, and lungs (often via Wind-Heat).
- **Hallmark signs:** high fever, red face, thirst for cold drinks, irritability or restlessness, scant dark urine, dry constipated stool, red tongue with yellow coat, rapid pulse. Severe Fire produces bleeding (Heat forces Xue out of vessels), delirium ("Heat agitating the Shen"), and skin rashes.
- **Pattern affinity:** Heart (Shen disturbance), Lung (sore throat, productive yellow phlegm), Liver (red eyes, irritability).
- **Internal Heat** comes from Yin deficiency (false/empty Heat) or stagnation transforming to Heat (Liver Qi stagnation → Liver Fire). The distinction matters: empty Heat needs Yin nourishment, not heat-clearing.

### Damp (Shi)

Damp is **heavy, sticky, downward-leaning, and lingering.** It is the most stubborn of the evils — the maxim says _"Damp is like glue: it cannot be hurried away."_

- **Entry route:** feet and lower body; environmental humidity.
- **Hallmark signs:** subjective heaviness ("body feels like wet clothing"), brain fog, sluggishness, joint heaviness, oppressive chest, sticky bowel movements, thick greasy tongue coat, slippery pulse.
- **Pattern affinity:** Spleen first (Spleen "hates Damp"), then Lower Burner.
- **Combinations:** Damp-Cold (chronic joint pain, bloating, watery diarrhea) and Damp-Heat (UTIs, jaundice, eczema, dysentery) are extremely common.
- The full pathological cascade — Damp → Phlegm → masses — lives in [JinYe.md](JinYe.md).

### Dryness (Zao)

Dryness **depletes fluids**, especially in the Lung and skin. The autumn pathogen.

- **Entry route:** nose, mouth, lungs.
- **Hallmark signs:** dry cough without sputum, dry throat, dry nose with nosebleeds, dry skin, dry constipated stool, red tongue with little coating.
- **Pattern affinity:** Lung (which "hates dryness"), Large Intestine, and Stomach.
- **Cool Dryness vs Warm Dryness** — early autumn warm dryness mimics Wind-Heat; late autumn cool dryness mimics Wind-Cold. Treatment differs accordingly.
- **Internal Dryness** is essentially Yin deficiency with a different name; chronic, constitutional, and treated with fluid-generating tonics.

### Summer-Heat (Shu)

A seasonal pathogen unique to high summer (roughly the dog days). Strictly external — there is no "internal Summer-Heat." It often combines with Damp because humidity dominates the season.

- **Entry route:** through prolonged sun exposure or heat stress.
- **Hallmark signs:** high fever, profuse sweating that does _not_ relieve the fever, intense thirst, exhaustion, scant dark urine. Severe cases produce sunstroke with sudden collapse, "Heat collapsing the Shen."
- **Summer-Heat with Damp** is the more common form in humid climates — sticky sweating, gastrointestinal upset, fever, heavy limbs.
- Treatment combines clearing Heat and generating fluids; the classical formula **Qing Shu Yi Qi Tang** addresses the combined Heat-fluid loss.

## Combined patterns

Pure single-evil patterns are rare. The frequent clinical combinations:

| Pattern   | Presentation                                                           | Treatment direction       |
| --------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Wind-Cold | Acute onset, chills > fever, clear nasal discharge, occipital headache | Warm and release exterior |
| Wind-Heat | Acute onset, fever > chills, sore throat, yellow nasal discharge       | Cool and release exterior |
| Wind-Damp | Migratory joint pain with heaviness                                    | Disperse Wind, drain Damp |
| Damp-Heat | UTI, jaundice, eczema, sticky bowel movements                          | Clear Heat, drain Damp    |
| Cold-Damp | Chronic joint pain worse in cold weather, bloating                     | Warm Yang, drain Damp     |

## Depth of penetration: the Wei-Qi-Ying-Xue framework

External pathogens that aren't expelled at the surface penetrate inward through four progressive levels — the **Wen Bing** (Warm Disease) school's contribution to TCM pathology:

1. **Wei level** — exterior; defensive Qi still engaged. Fever, chills, surface symptoms. Treatable with release-the-exterior formulas.
2. **Qi level** — pathogen has moved into the deeper organs but stays at the level of Qi (function). High fever, sweating, thirst, no chills. Treated with clearing Heat at the Qi level.
3. **Ying (Nutritive) level** — pathogen has reached the Nutritive Qi that flows with Blood. Persistent fever, mental restlessness, possible faint rash, tongue red without coating.
4. **Xue (Blood) level** — deepest; pathogen attacks Blood directly. Bleeding (nosebleeds, blood in urine/stool), severe Shen disturbance, dark purple tongue. Life-threatening.

Treatment shifts dramatically by level — the same Wind-Heat that needed mint and chrysanthemum at the Wei level needs deep-cooling herbs like Sheng Di and Mu Dan Pi at the Xue level.

## Modern parallels

The Liu Yin framework anticipates several modern concepts at functional resolution:

- **Infectious disease.** Pre-bacteriology, TCM categorized infections by their phenomenology — fevers with chills (Cold), fevers with thirst (Heat), illnesses with heavy fluid retention (Damp). The categories often map onto specific pathogen types (viral upper respiratory = typically Wind-Cold/Wind-Heat; gastrointestinal infections = typically Damp).
- **Climate-related illness.** Seasonal asthma flares, winter joint pain, summer heat exhaustion — TCM's external-evil framework directly anticipated chronotherapy and seasonal medicine.
- **Autoimmune phenomena.** Some integrative practitioners read autoimmune flares as "internal Wind" — sudden, shifting, multi-organ — though the framework was not built to address autoimmunity specifically.
- **Inflammatory patterns.** Heat-and-Damp patterns track inflammatory and infectious conditions with notable clinical overlap.

The framework is best read as a _phenomenological taxonomy of how the body shows illness_, not as a competing theory of microbial etiology. It can guide treatment effectively alongside biomedical diagnostics.
