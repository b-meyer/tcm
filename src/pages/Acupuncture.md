## Overview

Acupuncture and moxibustion together form the **first branch** of the Five Branches of TCM Treatment (see [index.md](index.md)) and the modality most widely recognized in the West. Acupuncture inserts ultra-thin needles into [acupoints](Jingmai.md) along the meridian network; moxibustion burns mugwort (_Ai Ye_) at or near the skin to warm those same points and pathways. Both manipulate the flow of [Qi](Qi.md) and [Blood](Xue.md) through the channels, guided by the [Zang-Fu](ZangFu.md) pattern diagnosed for that patient.

## How it works

In TCM terms, a needle at an acupoint locally moves Qi at the surface of a meridian; the practitioner chooses points to either **drain** excess (Heat, Phlegm, stagnation) or **tonify** deficiency (Qi, Blood, Yin, Yang) along that channel and through it to the connected organ. Moxibustion adds Yang and warmth — it is used wherever Cold, Dampness, or Yang deficiency dominates. The point selection is downstream of the pattern: [Ba Gang](BaGang.md) and [Si Zhen](SiZhen.md) tell the practitioner what to needle, the meridian network tells them where.

- Pain management — the entry-point application in Western adoption (back pain, headaches, joint conditions)

- Emotional patterns — Liver Qi stagnation (Liver 3 / Taichong is canonical), grief via Lung points

- Five-Element vs TCM-style acupuncture — the two dominant clinical schools
