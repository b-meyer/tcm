## Overview

The Lung in Traditional Chinese Medicine encompasses far more than the gas-exchange organ of Western physiology. Capitalized to mark the distinction, the **Lung** is the **Prime Minister** — second only to the [Heart](Heart.md) in the imperial metaphor — and governs the body's relationship with the outside world. It controls respiration, generates [Wei Qi (Defensive Qi)](Qi.md) with the [Spleen](Spleen.md), and rules the skin and body hair as the body's first boundary.

## Primary function

The Lung governs Qi and respiration. It _disperses_ Wei Qi outward to the skin (defending against external pathogens — see [LiuYin.md](LiuYin.md)) and _descends_ Qi and fluids downward to the [Kidney](Kidney.md) for storage and excretion. Failures of dispersing cause exterior weakness; failures of descending cause coughs, wheezing, and fluid retention in the upper body.

## Common patterns

Three pattern triad — the canonical Lung pathologies a practitioner screens for first.

- **Lung Qi deficiency** — Weak voice, spontaneous daytime sweating (Wei Qi cannot hold the pores), frequent colds, shortness of breath on exertion, pale tongue with thin white coat.
- **Lung Yin deficiency** — Dry unproductive cough, dry sore throat worse in the evening, red-edged tongue with little coating. Common after chronic smoking, dry climate, or febrile illness.
- **Wind invasion (Wind-Cold or Wind-Heat)** — Wind enters via the nose, back of neck, and pores. Wind-Cold: chills and occipital stiffness; Wind-Heat: sore throat and fever. Both begin as Lung patterns before penetrating deeper. See [LiuYin.md](LiuYin.md) for the full external-pathogen framework.
