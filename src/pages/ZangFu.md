# Zang Fu

## Overview

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), Zang Fu theory forms the core foundation of physiology, pathology, and diagnosis. Unlike Western medicine — which defines organs by cellular structure and physical boundaries — TCM views the Zang Fu as functional, interconnected systems. An "organ" in TCM encompasses not just a physical structure, but also its corresponding energetic pathway (meridian), emotional state, sensory aperture, and environmental element.

## The core split: Zang (Yin) vs. Fu (Yang)

The internal organs are divided into two distinct, Yin-Yang paired groups based on their physiological behavior.

### The 5 Zang organs (Yin)

The Zang organs are solid. Their primary purpose is to manufacture, transform, and store vital substances — Qi, Blood, Essence (Jing), and Body Fluids. Because they store life-sustaining energies, they are protected deep within the body and operate continuously.

- **Heart (Xin)** — Governs blood circulation and houses the Shen (mind/spirit). Dictates mental clarity and sleep.
- **Liver (Gan)** — Ensures the smooth flow of Qi, regulates emotions, and stores blood (vital for menstruation).
- **Spleen (Pi)** — The main organ of digestion. Transforms food into Qi and Blood and transports it throughout the body.
- **Lung (Fei)** — Governs respiration and dominates the body's boundary (the skin and immune system / Defensive Qi).
- **Kidney (Shen)** — Stores Pre-Natal Jing (essence/DNA), governs growth, reproduction, and water metabolism. _Note: "Shen" here is the Kidney organ; a different Chinese character with the same pinyin spelling refers to Spirit-Shen, covered in [Shen.md](Shen.md). See the [pinyin glossary](index.md#pinyin-disambiguation)._

### The 6 Fu organs (Yang)

The Fu organs are hollow. Their primary purpose is to receive, break down, transport, and eliminate food and waste. They constantly fill and empty, interacting directly with the exterior environment.

- **Stomach, Small Intestine, Large Intestine, Gallbladder, Urinary Bladder** — Handle the mechanical processing of nutrients and fluids.
- **San Jiao (Triple Burner)** — A uniquely TCM concept. Not a physical organ; an invisible pathway that regulates fluid metabolism and temperature throughout the upper, middle, and lower cavities of the torso.

> **A note on the Pericardium.** For the purposes of the 12 primary meridian channels, TCM also recognizes the **Pericardium** as a sixth Zang organ paired with the San Jiao as its Fu counterpart. The five-organ Zang list above reflects the older Five Phases framework ([WuXing.md](WuXing.md)), while the six-organ system (Pericardium + the five core Zang) is what gives the meridian system in [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md) its 12 symmetrical channels. See the [5-vs-6 Zang reconciliation](index.md#the-5-vs-6-zang-reconciliation) for the unified view.

## The Five Zang in depth

### Heart in depth

The Heart (Xin) is the Emperor: governs Blood, houses Shen (see [Shen.md](Shen.md)), opens to the tongue, emotion is joy (agitation or mania in excess). Surface pathway: the Heart channel runs from the chest down the inner arm to the little finger (see [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)). Three key patterns:

- **Heart Fire** — Palpitations, mouth sores, vivid disturbing dreams, red tongue tip, rapid pulse; Heat agitates the Shen.
- **Heart Blood deficiency** — Difficulty falling asleep, poor memory, mild anxiety, pale thin tongue; often co-arises with Spleen deficiency (see combined patterns below).
- **Heart Yin deficiency** — "Hollow" restlessness after midnight, low-grade night sweats, red tongue with little coating; a deeper depletion of the Heart's cooling aspect.

### Liver in depth

The Liver (Gan) is the General: smooths Qi flow, stores Blood (see [Xue.md](Xue.md)), governs sinews, opens to the eyes, emotion is anger or frustration (see [QiQing.md](QiQing.md) for emotional causation) — the quintessential stress organ in modern TCM. Surface pathway: the Liver channel runs up the inner leg through the genital region to the costal margin (see [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)). Three central patterns:

- **Liver Qi stagnation** — The most commonly diagnosed pattern; rib-side tension, mood swings, _plum-pit Qi_ in the throat, menstrual irregularity, wiry pulse. Chronic stress is the chief driver.
- **Liver Blood deficiency** — Dry eyes and nails, scanty menstruation, tendon cramps, pale tongue; frequently co-arises with Heart Blood deficiency.
- **Liver Yang rising** — Insufficient Kidney or Liver Yin fails to anchor Yang upward; throbbing temporal headache, dizziness, red face. Can progress toward Liver Wind.

### Spleen in depth

The Spleen (Pi) is the central digestive engine: _transforms_ food into Qi and Blood (hua) and _transports_ them upward and outward (yun). Also holds Blood in vessels and governs muscle tone. Opens to the mouth; emotion is worry — chronic overthinking directly taxes Spleen function. Surface pathway: the Spleen channel runs up the inner leg through the abdomen to the chest (see [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)). Key patterns:

- **Spleen Qi deficiency** — Fatigue after eating, loose stools, bloating, pale tongue with scalloped edges, weak pulse. The sinking variant underlies organ prolapse.
- **Spleen Yang deficiency** — Colder, deeper form: pronounced cold limbs, watery stools, aversion to cold food; develops from prolonged Qi deficiency or Kidney Yang failing to warm digestion.
- **Damp accumulation** — Unmetabolized fluids collect as pathological Dampness; heavy foggy-headed sensation, thick greasy tongue coat, sluggish digestion. Damp can congeal further into Phlegm.

### Lung in depth

The Lung (Fei) governs respiration and generates Wei Qi with the Spleen — the body's first immune defense (see [Qi.md](Qi.md)). Controls the skin, opens to the nose; emotion is grief, which when unresolved depletes Lung Qi. Surface pathway: the Lung channel runs from the chest down the outer arm to the thumb (see [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)). Three main patterns:

- **Lung Qi deficiency** — Weak voice, spontaneous daytime sweating (Wei Qi cannot hold the pores), frequent colds, shortness of breath on exertion, pale tongue with thin white coat.
- **Lung Yin deficiency** — Dry unproductive cough, dry sore throat worse in the evening, red-edged tongue with little coating. Common after chronic smoking, dry climate, or febrile illness.
- **Wind invasion (Wind-Cold or Wind-Heat)** — Wind enters via the nose, back of neck, and pores. Wind-Cold: chills and occipital stiffness; Wind-Heat: sore throat and fever. Both begin as Lung patterns before penetrating deeper. See [LiuYin.md](LiuYin.md) for the full external-pathogen framework.

### Kidney in depth

The Kidney is the root of life: stores Pre-Natal Jing (see [Jing.md](Jing.md)), governs water metabolism, produces marrow (filling bones and brain), and _grasps Qi_ by anchoring Lung inhalation — a grasping failure underlies many chronic asthmas. Opens to the ears; emotion is fear. Surface pathway: the Kidney channel runs from the sole of the foot up the inner leg to the chest (see [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)). Three defining patterns:

- **Kidney Yang deficiency** — Deep fatigue, cold lower back and knees, copious clear urine, dawn diarrhea, pale swollen tongue with white coat. Kidney Yang is the root of all bodily Yang, so Spleen and Heart are often secondarily affected.
- **Kidney Yin deficiency** — Low back ache, afternoon flushing, night sweats, scanty dark urine, red tongue with little coating. Depleted Kidney Water fails to cool Heart Fire — a primary driver of the Heart-Kidney axis pattern below.
- **Kidney Essence depletion (Jing xu)** — Premature aging, early-onset sexual dysfunction, infertility, premature graying, cognitive decline. Jing replenishment is slow; tonifying herbs (He Shou Wu, Rou Cong Rong) and lifestyle conservation are central to treatment.

## The Heart-Kidney axis

Heart is Fire and Kidney is Water (see [WuXing.md](WuXing.md)). In health, Kidney Yin rises to cool the Heart while Heart Fire descends to warm the Kidneys — a dialogue called _Heart-Kidney communication_ (xin shen xiang jiao). When Kidney Yin is too depleted to rise, Heart Fire runs unchecked: the classic picture is **insomnia, restless dreaming, palpitations, night sweats, and persistent low-grade anxiety**. The Shen cannot settle without Yin's anchor (see [Shen.md](Shen.md)). Treatment nourishes Kidney Yin while clearing Heart Fire — _Tian Wang Bu Xin Dan_ is canonical. Chronic overwork and late nights are typical antecedents.

## Common combined patterns

Organ systems rarely fail in isolation; the Five Phases cycles ([WuXing.md](WuXing.md)) propagate dysfunction across neighbors. Three combinations are especially common:

- **Liver overacting on the Spleen (Gan pi bu he).** Stagnant Liver Qi attacks adjacent Spleen, disrupting digestion — stress-triggered alternating bowel habits, cramping pain worsening with emotion, bloating, irritability. The TCM pattern most often mapped to IBS; treated with _Xiao Yao San_.
- **Heart-Spleen Blood deficiency.** The Spleen builds Blood, the Heart houses Shen within it (see [Xue.md](Xue.md)). Joint depletion produces insomnia, palpitations, anxiety, poor appetite, and pale tongue as an interlocked cluster. _Gui Pi Tang_ is canonical.
- **Lung-Kidney failing to grasp Qi.** Lung sends Qi down, Kidney holds it; when Kidney Yang or Jing falters, Qi floats unanchored — breathlessness worse on exertion, unsatisfying inhalation, low back weakness. Underlies chronic asthma in elderly or constitutionally weak patients.

## The Organ Clock connection

TCM maps each of the 12 principal meridians to a 2-hour peak-Qi window — the _Organ Clock_ (ziwu liuzhu). A symptom that recurs reliably at the same time of day is read as a pointer to the organ peaking in that window. The full 12-channel table lives in [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md); selected diagnostic correlations:

| Recurring symptom time | Peak organ  | Typical implication                                                               |
| ---------------------- | ----------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1 AM – 3 AM            | Liver       | Waking frustrated or unable to return to sleep; Liver Qi stagnation               |
| 3 AM – 5 AM            | Lung        | Pre-dawn coughing fits or night sweats; Lung Qi or Yin deficiency                 |
| 9 AM – 11 AM           | Spleen      | Mid-morning energy crash or bloating after breakfast; Spleen Qi deficiency        |
| 3 PM – 5 PM            | Bladder     | Mid-afternoon slump at the Bladder/Kidney transition; Bladder-Kidney Qi depletion |
| 11 PM – 1 AM           | Gallbladder | Difficulty falling asleep before midnight; Gallbladder Qi deficiency              |

## Why Zang Fu is the basis for TCM practice

### Pattern diagnosis (Bian Zheng) and treatment

A TCM practitioner rarely treats a symptom in isolation. A patient presenting with insomnia is not given a generic sleep herb; the practitioner maps the Zang Fu landscape first: is it Heart Fire (vivid dreams, red tongue tip), Heart-Kidney axis failure (night sweats, anxiety), or Liver Qi stagnation (waking frustrated at 3 AM per the Organ Clock)? Each pattern points to a different organ system and demands a different herbal formula or acupoint selection.

The Zang Fu organs are connected to the body surface via meridian channels (see [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)). Distress in an organ manifests along its meridian — chronic outer-thigh pain often maps to the Gallbladder meridian, signaling an underlying Liver/Gallbladder imbalance. Acupuncturists use surface points to either drain excess (heat, phlegm) or tonify a deficient organ, always guided by the Zang Fu pattern beneath.
