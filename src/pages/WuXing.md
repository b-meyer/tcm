# Wu Xing — The Five Phases

## Overview

Wu Xing, often translated as the "Five Elements," is a foundation stone of Traditional Chinese Medicine. A more accurate translation, however, is **Five Phases** or **Five Movements**. In the West, "elements" usually means static building blocks of matter (the ancient Greek earth, air, fire, and water). In Chinese philosophy, _Xing_ means "to walk" or "to move." Wu Xing therefore represents five dynamic, shifting phases of Qi that describe how the universe — and the human body — functions, changes, and maintains balance.

## Historical development

Early lists of five materials appear in the _Shang Shu_ without cyclic logic. **Zou Yan** (c. 305–240 BCE) of the Jixia Academy was the pivotal systematizer, weaving Yin/Yang cosmology and five-phase vocabulary into a theory of dynastic succession. The Han-era _Huangdi Neijing_ then embedded the framework in physiology, pairing each phase with organs, emotions, flavors, tissues, and climates. Wu Xing and Yin/Yang are inseparable: Yin/Yang describes polarity and rhythm; Wu Xing describes how five categories transform across time. See [YinYang.md](YinYang.md) for the foundational dualism.

## The matrix of correspondences

TCM maps every aspect of human physiology, emotion, and environment to one of the five phases. The human body is treated as a microcosm of nature: when the seasons change outside, a corresponding change happens inside. For emotions as causative agents in their own right, see [QiQing.md](QiQing.md).

| Phase | Yin organ (Zang) | Yang organ (Fu) | Season      | Emotion           | Flavor  | Tissue          |
| ----- | ---------------- | --------------- | ----------- | ----------------- | ------- | --------------- |
| Wood  | Liver            | Gallbladder     | Spring      | Anger / Drive     | Sour    | Tendons / Eyes  |
| Fire  | Heart            | Small Intestine | Summer      | Joy / Anxiety     | Bitter  | Blood vessels   |
| Earth | Spleen           | Stomach         | Late Summer | Worry / Pondering | Sweet   | Muscles / Flesh |
| Metal | Lung             | Large Intestine | Autumn      | Grief / Sadness   | Pungent | Skin / Hair     |
| Water | Kidney           | Urinary Bladder | Winter      | Fear / Willpower  | Salty   | Bones           |

_Wu Xing uses **5 Zang organs**, one per phase. The 12-meridian system in [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md) adds the **Pericardium** and **San Jiao (Triple Burner)** as a sixth Yin/Yang pair, which sit outside this 5-phase framework. ## The Five Phases in depth

### Wood

Wood governs spring: _ascending and spreading_, the push of a young tree toward light. Its organs are the **Liver** and **Gallbladder** ([ZangFu.md](ZangFu.md)) — the Liver smooths Qi and Blood, the Gallbladder holds the capacity for decision. Wood is the planning faculty: creativity and ambition balanced; irritability and rib-side tension when blocked. Supportive practices favour dispersal — lateral stretching, sour foods in moderation, movement that releases constraint.

### Fire

Fire governs summer: _rising and radiating_, maximum expansion. Its organs are the **Heart** and **Small Intestine** ([ZangFu.md](ZangFu.md)) — the Heart seats Shen ([Shen.md](Shen.md)) and presides over the pulse. Balanced Fire is warmth and relational ease; excess is anxiety, racing thought, insomnia, red tongue tip; deficiency is flat affect and withdrawal. Supportive practices: bitter foods to clear Heart heat, meditative stillness, limiting stimulation in peak summer.

### Earth

Earth governs late summer — the pivot at the cycle's center. Its movement is _stabilizing and transforming_, the composting quality of soil. Its organs are the **Spleen** and **Stomach** ([ZangFu.md](ZangFu.md)): Spleen extracts nutritive essence into Qi and Blood; Stomach receives and ripens intake. Earth's emotion is _yi_ — pensiveness that in excess becomes worry, fatigue, and dampness (see [JinYe.md](JinYe.md) for the Phlegm/Damp pathology that follows). Supportive practices: warm cooked food, naturally sweet whole foods (root vegetables, millet, squash), gentle post-meal movement.

### Metal

Metal governs autumn: _inward and descending_, contraction and harvest. Its organs are the **Lung** and **Large Intestine** ([ZangFu.md](ZangFu.md)) — Lung governs respiration and Wei Qi ([Qi.md](Qi.md)), Large Intestine completes release. Metal's emotion is grief and appropriate letting go: balanced gives discernment and clear boundaries; deficient gives chronic grief, respiratory susceptibility, dry skin, constipation. Supportive practices: diaphragmatic breathing, pungent foods (ginger, garlic, radish), intentional release.

### Water

Water governs winter: _sinking and consolidating_, the stillness of groundwater. Its organs are the **Kidney** and **Urinary Bladder** ([ZangFu.md](ZangFu.md)) — the Kidney is the root of Yin and Yang and the storehouse of Jing ([Jing.md](Jing.md)), governing bone, marrow, brain, and reproduction. Water's emotion spans fear to willpower: balanced is quiet courage and endurance; depleted is chronic fear and exhaustion unresolved by rest. Characteristic signs: lower-back weakness, hearing decline. Supportive practices: sleep before midnight, moderately salty foods (seaweed, miso, black beans), Kidney-tonifying Qigong at the lumbar.

## The dynamic cycles

The phases never exist in isolation; they are locked in a continuous dance of checks and balances. TCM diagnoses health based on whether these relationships are harmonious.

### The Generating Cycle (Sheng)

Also known as the **Mother-Son relationship**, this is a cycle of nourishment and creation. Each phase promotes and births the next:

- Wood burns to create Fire.
- Fire leaves behind ash to create Earth.
- Earth compresses to form Metal (minerals).
- Metal enriches and condenses Water.
- Water nourishes plants to grow Wood.

**Clinical application.** If a patient has weak Heart energy (Fire), a TCM practitioner might look to treat the Liver (Wood) — the "mother" organ — to help feed and strengthen the Heart.

### The Controlling Cycle (Ke)

Also known as the **Grandparent-Grandchild relationship**, this is a system of checks and balances that prevents any single element from growing too powerful:

- Wood (roots) binds and controls Earth.
- Earth dams and absorbs Water.
- Water extinguishes Fire.
- Fire melts and shapes Metal.
- Metal (an axe) chops Wood.

**Clinical application.** If Wood becomes hyperactive, it can over-control and crush Earth. Severe emotional stress or anger (Liver/Wood) often directly attacks digestion (Spleen/Stomach/Earth), leading to stress-induced ulcers or IBS.

## Pathological cycles (when things go wrong)

When the body's internal ecology breaks down, the cycles warp into pathological states:

- **Overacting (Cheng).** An element is overgrown and excessively crushes the element it is supposed to gently control (e.g., a hyperactive Liver completely shutting down the Spleen's digestive fire).
- **Insulting / Counteracting (Wu).** A subordinate element rebels against its controller. For example, if Metal (Lungs) is extremely weak, Wood (Liver) may "insult" it, manifesting as emotional stress causing asthma or breathing difficulties.

## Wu Xing in clinical practice

A TCM practitioner does not just look at an isolated symptom like a headache; they look for the underlying Wu Xing pattern.

- **Diagnosis.** Pulse, tongue, complexion (e.g., a greenish tint points to Wood/Liver issues), and voice quality are all mapped to a disrupted elemental cycle.
- **Treatment.** Acupuncture points are categorized by Wu Xing properties. If a patient has too much "Fire" (insomnia, racing thoughts, red tongue), a practitioner might needle a "Water" point on a specific meridian to help extinguish the excess heat.
- **Herbalism and diet.** Herbal formulas are explicitly designed using the five flavors. A patient with chronic dampness and fatigue (Earth deficiency) is given herbs and foods that are warm and naturally sweet to tonify the Spleen.

Wu Xing treats the body like a living ecosystem. The goal is not to eradicate a symptom with a blunt tool but to act as a master gardener — adjusting the water, clearing the weeds, regulating the heat — until the entire system balances itself.

## Dietary application of the five flavors

TCM treats food in the same energetic language it uses for herbs. Each of the five flavors enters and supports its corresponding phase:

| Flavor  | Phase | Action                                              | Examples                             |
| ------- | ----- | --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Sour    | Wood  | Astringes, consolidates; supports Liver/Gallbladder | Vinegar, lemon, plum, hawthorn       |
| Bitter  | Fire  | Clears heat, dries dampness; supports Heart/S.I.    | Dandelion greens, bitter melon       |
| Sweet   | Earth | Tonifies, harmonizes; supports Spleen/Stomach       | Root vegetables, millet, rice, dates |
| Pungent | Metal | Disperses, circulates; supports Lung/L.I.           | Ginger, onion, garlic, radish        |
| Salty   | Water | Softens, moistens; supports Kidney/Bladder          | Seaweed, miso, black beans, walnuts  |

**Excess injures before it nourishes.** Too much sour over-constrains Liver Qi and, via the Controlling Cycle, weakens the Spleen; excess salt burdens the Kidneys and can suppress Heart function. A balanced meal includes all five flavors in rough proportion.

**Seasonal eating.** Classical guidance tilts dietary emphasis with each season: more sour in spring (Liver); bitter in summer (Heart heat); warm-sweet in late summer (Spleen); pungent in autumn (Lung); warming and lightly salty in winter (Kidney reserves). No single flavor dominates; all five should be present, with the seasonal tilt as a gentle corrective rather than a strict rule.
