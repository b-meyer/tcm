## Overview

**Dietary therapy** is the **fourth branch** of the Five Branches of TCM Treatment — food prescribed by constitution and pattern. In TCM, the line between food and medicine is fuzzy: many herbs are foods (ginger, jujube, goji, cinnamon), and every food is classified along the same axes as herbs. The cooking method matters as much as the ingredient — the same vegetable shifts from cooling (raw) to neutral (steamed) to warming (stir-fried with ginger). This branch is the one most directly under the patient's daily control, which is why it's framed as both prevention and treatment.

## How it works

Each food is profiled along axes that mirror the [herbal](Herbs.md) framework:

- **Temperature** — Hot, Warm, Neutral, Cool, Cold (independent of literal serving temperature; cucumber is Cool even when stewed).
- **Flavor** — the [Five Flavors](WuXing.md) (Sour, Bitter, Sweet, Pungent, Salty), each entering the corresponding phase's organ pair.
- **Channel affinity** — which [organ system](ZangFu.md) the food primarily nourishes or drains.
- **Action** — tonifies Qi/Blood/Yin/Yang, moves stagnation, resolves Dampness, etc.

Diagnosis (via [Ba Gang](BaGang.md) and [Si Zhen](SiZhen.md)) drives the prescription: a Spleen Qi deficient patient gets warm, cooked, sweet foods and avoids cold raw salads; a Yin deficient patient gets moistening foods (pears, tofu, congee) and avoids drying spices. Dietary therapy is rarely the sole intervention but it underwrites everything else.
