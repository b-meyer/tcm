## Overview

The Spleen in Traditional Chinese Medicine is not the small filtering organ of Western anatomy. Capitalized to mark the distinction, the **Spleen** is the **central digestive engine** of the body — the organ that transforms food and drink into [Qi](Qi.md) and [Xue (Blood)](Xue.md), and transports them outward to nourish every tissue. Together with the [Stomach](Stomach.md) it forms the Earth-phase pair, the post-natal source of all acquired vitality.

## Primary function

The Spleen _transforms_ food into Qi and Blood (_hua_) and _transports_ them upward and outward (_yun_). It also **holds Blood within the vessels** (a failure here underlies easy bruising and heavy menstrual bleeding) and governs muscle tone and the limbs.

## Common patterns

Three pattern triad — the canonical Spleen pathologies a practitioner screens for first.

- **Spleen Qi deficiency** — Fatigue after eating, loose stools, bloating, pale tongue with scalloped edges, weak pulse. The sinking variant (_Spleen Qi sinking_) underlies organ prolapse.
- **Spleen Yang deficiency** — Colder, deeper form: pronounced cold limbs, watery stools, aversion to cold food; develops from prolonged Qi deficiency or [Kidney](Kidney.md) Yang failing to warm digestion.
- **Damp accumulation** — Unmetabolized fluids collect as pathological Dampness; heavy foggy-headed sensation, thick greasy tongue coat, sluggish digestion. Damp can congeal further into Phlegm (see [JinYe.md](JinYe.md)).
