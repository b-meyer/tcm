# Qigong

## Overview

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, **Qigong** is far more than gentle exercise; it is one of the core branches of treatment and a pillar of preventative health. While acupuncture and herbal medicine are practitioner-applied, qigong is a self-care practice — the patient actively regulates their own health.

The word combines **Qi** (vital energy — see [Qi.md](Qi.md)) and **Gong** (skill, work, cultivation through dedication). Qigong is therefore the **skillful cultivation and manipulation of the body's vital energy**.

## Qigong as a pillar of TCM

Qigong stands among the **[Five Branches of TCM Treatment](index.md#the-five-branches-of-tcm-treatment)** — the canonical toolkit a classical practitioner reaches for. The other four are all practitioner-applied, and each has its own page in Additional Reading:

1. **[Acupuncture & Moxibustion](Acupuncture.md)** — needles and warmth at acupoints along the [meridian network](Jingmai.md).
2. **[Herbal Medicine](Herbs.md)** — formulas built from substances classified by temperature, flavor, and channel.
3. **[Tui Na](TuiNa.md)** — therapeutic massage along the meridian pathways.
4. **[Dietary Therapy](Dietary.md)** — food prescribed by constitution and pattern.
5. **Qigong** — _this page_.

Qigong is the **fifth branch in the canonical listing** — and the **only one the patient owns directly**, practiced on themselves, daily, without a practitioner present. The other four treat what has already manifested; qigong is the work the patient does so that less ever has to manifest. It is preventative medicine in the literal sense: the _Huangdi Neijing_ warns that treating disease after manifestation is like digging a well when already thirsty. Among the five, qigong is what makes TCM as much a philosophy of daily life as a clinical practice.

## Origins and lineages

The term "qigong" was codified in the 20th century, but the practices are ancient — the _Mawangdui_ silk manuscripts (c. 168 BCE) catalogue Dao Yin exercises that are direct ancestors of modern forms like Ba Duan Jin. Four lineage streams shaped today's practice:

- **Daoist (Dao Jia).** The most influential root. Yangsheng (nourishing life) and Neidan (inner alchemy) refine the Three Treasures (see [Jing.md](Jing.md), [Shen.md](Shen.md)) toward longevity and spiritual transcendence.
- **Buddhist (Fo Jia).** Adapted through Chan (Zen) monasteries; prioritizes stillness. The Yi Jin Jing is traditionally — though likely apocryphally — attributed to Bodhidharma teaching it to Shaolin monks.
- **Medical (Yi Gong).** Clinical TCM; prescribed routines treat or prevent disease. Hua Tuo formalized this stream with the Wu Qin Xi; it dominates the modern research literature.
- **Martial (Wu Gong).** Builds tendon resilience and explosive Qi release. Tai Chi Chuan, Xing Yi, and Bagua are wu gong as fighting systems.

Confucian self-cultivation and Shen Gong (pure spirit work) are smaller adjacent streams; most schools blend several lineages.

## Theoretical frameworks

Qigong operates on the same physiological and energetic principles as acupuncture.

### The meridian system (Jing Luo)

TCM dictates that Qi flows through a network of invisible pathways called meridians, which connect the surface of the body to the internal organs.

- **The cause of illness.** Disease or pain occurs when Qi becomes stagnant, blocked, deficient, or hyperactive.
- **The qigong solution.** The specific stretches, twists, and dynamic movements of qigong physically target and open these meridian pathways. Stretching the arms overhead and outward, for example, can directly stimulate the Lung and Large Intestine meridians to boost immunity.

### The Three Treasures (San Bao)

Qigong aims to harmonize the three fundamental substances of human life — **Jing** (essence, preserved by posture and structural alignment), **Qi** (vital energy, cultivated via breathwork and movement), and **Shen** (spirit, steadied by meditative focus). For the canonical Treasures framing, see the [Three Treasures framing](index.md#the-three-treasures-san-bao).

### The Three Adjustments (San Tiao)

Every qigong style, whether dynamic (moving) or passive (still), requires the harmonious integration of three components:

| Component             | Pinyin    | TCM Function                                                                                                                                   |
| --------------------- | --------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Regulating the Body   | Tiao Shen | Correct, aligned posture. Relaxing the muscles so the meridians are not physically compressed, allowing Qi and blood to flow smoothly.         |
| Regulating the Breath | Tiao Xi   | Slow, deep diaphragmatic breathing. Pulls fresh "Gu Qi" (energy from the air) into the lungs and activates the parasympathetic nervous system. |
| Regulating the Mind   | Tiao Xin  | Focused intent (_Yi_). A central rule of TCM: "Where the mind goes, the Qi follows." Visualization directs energy to where it is needed.       |

## Forms and traditions

Traditional qigong has thousands of recorded branches, but they generally fall into two categories defined by whether the body is moving or still:

- **Dynamic qigong (Dong Gong).** Practiced through deliberate, flowing movements, twists, and stretches that physically open the meridian pathways.
- **Static qigong (Jing Gong).** Practiced through held postures, seated meditation, or visualization, where Qi is cultivated through stillness rather than motion.

A handful of standardized forms have become the canonical entry points to the tradition, and the Chinese Health Qigong Association formally codified several of them in the early 2000s for medical and educational use:

- **Ba Duan Jin (Eight Brocades).** Dynamic. Eight repetitive movements that stretch the major meridians and strengthen the musculoskeletal system. Dates back over 1,000 years and is the most heavily researched form.
- **Wu Qin Xi (Five Animal Frolics).** Dynamic. A set imitating the movement quality of the Tiger, Deer, Bear, Ape, and Bird; traditionally targeted at emotional balance and joint mobility.
- **Yi Jin Jing (Muscle/Tendon Changing Classic).** Dynamic. A more strenuous form historically associated with Shaolin training, used to build tendon strength and deep Qi.
- **Zhan Zhuang (Standing Meditation).** Static. Holding a single posture — most famously "holding a tree" — to cultivate structural alignment and internal power.

## Qigong vs Tai Chi

**Qigong is the category; Tai Chi Chuan is one specific form within it.** Tai Chi (Taijiquan — "Supreme Ultimate Fist") is a wu gong: Qigong principles expressed as a choreographed martial form with Yin/Yang dynamics (see [YinYang.md](YinYang.md)) baked into every posture. The relation is roughly jogging-to-exercise — every Tai Chi practice is qigong, but most qigong is not Tai Chi.

The West conflates them because the softer post-19th-century Yang style, which reached Western audiences, looks nearly identical to health qigong — slow, low-impact, breath-connected. The meaningful distinction is that Tai Chi forms have a fixed sequence with martial applications for every posture; generic qigong forms do not.

## Qigong and the diurnal cycle

TCM treats the human body as a microcosm of the natural world, and qigong is deliberately matched to the body's daily rhythm (see [YinYang.md](YinYang.md#yin-and-yang-in-time)). The morning is **Yang time** (rising energy, warmth, outward movement); the evening is **Yin time** (sinking energy, coolness, inward stillness). Practitioners traditionally choose expansive, upward, dynamic forms in the morning to clear stagnation and energize the body, and slow, downward, grounding forms in the evening to settle the Shen and prepare for sleep. The same form practiced at the wrong time of day can produce the opposite of its intended effect — vigorous practice close to bedtime, for example, is considered a classical cause of insomnia.

## Scientific research

Evidence quality varies — many early studies were small and unblinded — but several outcome areas have accumulated controlled-trial support:

- **Blood pressure** is the most consistently replicated finding; meta-analyses show meaningful systolic reductions in hypertensive populations, attributed to sympathetic down-regulation. Ba Duan Jin is the most-studied form.
- **HRV** rises consistently with practice, a clean convergence point between Tiao Xi (slow diaphragmatic breathing) and parasympathetic activation.
- **Cortisol** drops and the stress response blunts after multi-week interventions, suggesting HPA-axis modulation; sample sizes remain small.
- **Balance and fall prevention** in adults over 60 is well-supported, particularly for Ba Duan Jin and Zhan Zhuang.

Immune enhancement and Qi emission (wai gong) claims remain poorly supported by controlled evidence. Persistent limitation: qigong cannot be blinded, so subjective outcomes inflate.

## Medical vs. personal qigong

In clinical TCM settings, qigong is divided into two categories:

- **Internal qigong (self-practice).** The patient is prescribed specific movements, breathing techniques, or meditations tailored to their TCM diagnosis. A patient with "Liver Qi Stagnation" due to stress, for instance, might be given specific twisting exercises to release tension in the flanks.
- **External qigong (Qi emission).** A highly trained qigong master or doctor directs their own cultivated energy into the patient's body — without physical touch or via light touch — to purge stagnation or replenish deficiencies. Functions similarly to a needle-less acupuncture session.

## Common beginner pitfalls and contraindications

- **Over-breathing.** "Deep breathing" misread as breathing _more_ rather than _more slowly_. Rapid deep breathing drops arterial CO2 and produces dizziness; TCM frames this as scattering the Shen. Prioritize exhalation length over inhalation volume.
- **Forcing the Lower Dantian.** Bearing down with abdominal pressure stagnates Qi rather than circulating it. The Dantian develops through relaxed sustained practice, not muscular effort.
- **Timing.** Practicing within 30–60 minutes of a large meal competes with Spleen-Stomach digestion (see [ZangFu.md](ZangFu.md)); vigorous practice within 1–2 hours of bedtime is a classical cause of insomnia.
- **Zou Huo Ru Mo.** "Catching fire and entering demonhood" — adverse events from intensive or incorrectly guided practice, recognized in Chinese psychiatric literature as a culture-bound syndrome ranging from spontaneous heat and uncontrolled movement to paranoia and dissociation. Risk is negligible for standardized health forms at moderate pace; it becomes meaningful with unsupervised breath retention, intense neidan visualization, or prolonged retreat.

**Contraindications.** Active psychotic disorders, severe cardiovascular instability, recent abdominal surgery, and late-stage pregnancy all call for medical clearance. External qigong (Qi emission) should be sought only from qualified practitioners.
